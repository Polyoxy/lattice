# Ballroom — 1930s Foxtrot Production DNA Brief

Research input for the Lattice spec. Compiled 2026-07-17. Target: theater/hotel dance
orchestra playing strict-tempo foxtrot, 1930–1936 — piano-forward stride, walking upright
bass, brushes, string pads.

**Reliability key:** `cited` = a named source states it (URL given). `inferred` = a
deduction from cited evidence, reasoned in the text. `designed-space` = an aesthetic or
engineering choice made for the card, flagged as a decision rather than a fact. Where a
source is itself known only secondhand (e.g. a dissertation quoted inside a later paper),
that chain is stated rather than presented as a direct read.

## 1. Swing ratio at foxtrot tempi (104–126 bpm)

**Swing ratio vs. tempo — `cited`.** Friberg & Sundström's ride-cymbal study (TMH-QPSR
4/1997, KTH — the technical-report precursor to "Swing Ratios and Ensemble Timing in Jazz
Performance," *Music Perception* 19(3), 2002) states in its abstract: "the swing ratio can
be as high as 3.5:1 at comparatively slow tempi around 120 bpm... When the tempo is fast
the swing ratio reaches 1:1." Their published per-excerpt data lands four points inside
this brief's band: *My Funny Valentine* (Tony Williams, 112 bpm) 3.19:1; *I Thought About
You* (Williams, 119 bpm) 2.89:1; *Body and Soul* (Jack DeJohnette, 120 bpm) 2.25:1;
*Stella by Starlight* (Williams, 125 bpm) 3.23:1 — averaging ≈2.9:1 (≈74% swing). Rose
(1989 PhD dissertation, Univ. of Texas — known here only via Friberg & Sundström's
summary) measured 2.38:1 (≈70%) at 132 bpm across bass/piano/drums together, all three
"approximately the same."

**Ensemble swing in 1930s dance bands specifically — `inferred`.** No measured data on
1930s big-band or ballroom-orchestra swing ratios turned up in this pass. Every ratio
above is a 1958–1990 small-combo recording (Miles Davis/Coltrane-lineage rhythm
sections) — bebop-descended ride-cymbal phrasing is not the same discipline as a notated,
strict-tempo dance chart. Real gap.

**Recommended range — `designed-space`.** A ballroom foxtrot band plays for dancers'
footwork, not over a ride pattern — pull the target below the ≈74–78% measured ceiling.
Recommend **58–68% swing (≈1.4:1–2.1:1)** as default, ≈78% reserved as an optional "hot,"
more Basie-adjacent setting.

## 2. Micro-timing roles (bass, drums, piano)

**Magnitude — `cited`.** Prögler (1995, "Searching for Swing: Participatory Discrepancies
in the Jazz Rhythm Section," *Ethnomusicology* 39(1):21–54) measured drummer-to-bassist
offsets clustering **10–60 ms**, one bass/ridetap pairing averaging ~40 ms (range 4–80
ms). Butterfield ("Participatory Discrepancies and the Perception of Beats in Jazz,"
*Music Perception* 27(3):157–176, 2010 — sole author; no co-author named Ashley turned up
attached to this title) puts participatory discrepancies generally at "usually... less
than about 50 milliseconds."

**Direction — `cited`, contested.** Prögler found no universal rule: one drummer sat
consistently ahead of the bass, another consistently behind, "in about the same range of
ms." Rose (1989, via Friberg & Sundström 1997) found the opposite of the common
assumption at 132 bpm — drums furthest ahead, piano next, **bass third, i.e. furthest
behind** — of the three instruments measured. Butterfield's own perceptual experiments
then found "little evidence... in support of the [PD] framework" as swing's cause at all.

**Engine choice — `designed-space`.** Direction doesn't converge in the literature, so
stylize toward the pedagogically conventional read: walking bass anchored at/near the beat
(±5 ms jitter, no systematic lead/lag) as the reference dancers can trust, brushes/snare
comping 10–25 ms behind it for laid-back feel, feathered kick loosest of all (~±15–20 ms)
since it's felt-not-heard, not a timekeeping role.

## 3. Stride piano left hand

**Pattern and register — `cited`.** Standard "oom-pah": single bass note, octave, or
tenth on beats 1 & 3, chord on 2 & 4 (Wikipedia "Stride (music)"; MasterClass; Yamaha
Hub). MusicRadar's technique breakdown: "aim low with your fifth finger to hit the bass
note, then mov[e] quickly to the middle register to grab a chord" — bass low, chord in
"close position" mid-keyboard. James P. Johnson is credited with the "broken tenth,"
rolling a tenth interval in place of a plain bass note (Johnson, Waller, Hines, and Tatum
are the tenth-players named across sources).

**Tempo limits and ensemble simplification — `cited` + `inferred`.** No source gives a
hard BPM ceiling for full stride — "stride can be played at all tempos" per multiple
pedagogy sites, tenths "broken up" between both hands as a speed accommodation
(MusicRadar). But full alternating-bass stride "requires no rhythmic or harmonic
accompaniment, making it a technique used almost exclusively for [solo] piano" — in a
dance-band section, piano more commonly plays simplified stride-derived comping while
bass/guitar covers the "oom." **`designed-space`:** at 104–126 bpm (well within
full-stride's range), reserve true alternating-bass stride for piano breaks/intros and
default ensemble texture to 2-and-4 chord comping over the bass.

## 4. Walking bass construction

**Line-building rule — `cited`.** Standard pedagogy (Friedland's *Building Walking Bass
Lines*, Reid's *The Evolving Bassist* — both confirmed as root-based, building-block
method books via their own publisher descriptions; this pass could not access verbatim
rule text from either): root on beat 1, chord/scale tone on 2 and 3, chromatic or scalar
approach tone into the next root on beat 4.

**Two-feel vs. four-feel — `cited`.** Two-feel (root + fifth, half notes on 1 & 3) is the
older New Orleans-descended default; the modern four-to-the-bar walking line "emerged...
during the 1930s" as gut-strung, bowed tuba-doubling gave way to plucked string bass
(thisisjazz.com). Walter Page (Blue Devils, then Basie) is credited as "one of the first
bassists to play four beats to the bar," extending Pops Foster's New Orleans two-beat
lineage; Page, Jo Jones, Freddie Green, and Basie became the "All-American Rhythm
Section" (Wikipedia "Walter Page"). **`inferred`:** since four-feel only "emerged during"
the decade rather than opening it, a 1930–36-set ballroom band skews toward two-feel more
than a late-30s/Basie-era chart would.

**Register — `cited`.** Double bass strings tuned E1–A1–D2–G2; practical jazz walking range ≈E1–E3.

## 5. Brush technique vocabulary

**Snare pattern — `cited`.** The circular "swirl" (continuous directional-change sweep)
and the straight windshield-wiper sweep (hand still, forearm driving) both trace to Papa
Jo Jones, "one of the first drummers to promote the use of brushes" (Wikipedia "Jo
Jones"; DRUM! Magazine, "Brush Methods of the Masters"). Taps land on/around 2 & 4 with
the free hand while the sweep continues underneath.

**Feathering the bass drum — `cited`.** Very light quarter notes on the kick, "felt but
not heard" — maintains pulse and low end "without being a separate rhythm," origin "often
credited to Papa Jo Jones" (learnjazzdrums.co; cruiseshipdrummer.com).

**Hi-hat role — `cited` + `inferred`.** Through the 1920s the "sock cymbal"/"low-boy"
(8–14", roughly a foot off the floor) was foot-only, a "chick" on 2 & 4; a raised,
stick-playable design dates to ~1926 (Walberg & Auge). Jones (with Basie from 1934,
nationally prominent from their 1936 New York arrival) pioneered continuous open/close
ride patterns on the raised hi-hat, shifting timekeeping off the bass drum. Across this
brief's 1930–36 window that shift was just beginning, not yet standard across ballroom/
hotel rhythm sections generally.

**Chick Webb, for contrast — `cited`.** Webb's style ran opposite Jones' minimalism: 28"
bass drum played "free" (undamped), wide dynamic "shading" from silence to roar, press
rolls, rim shots, temple blocks (scottkfish.com). **`designed-space`:** a genteel
theater/ballroom card should default toward the Jones/brush end of that spectrum, not the Webb/Savoy-stomp end.

## 6. 1930s harmony vocabulary

**Tonic 6th chord — `cited`.** "By the late 1920s and especially the early 1930s... the
added sixth became the tonic chord of choice in jazz" (Martin, "On the Tonic Added-Sixth
Chord in Jazz," *Music Theory Online* 29(2), 2023) — McKinney's Cotton Pickers recorded
it from mid-1928; Lopez's 1933 piano method taught it under "Modern Harmony" as
professional-standard technique. Major-7th-as-tonic-color is a later, bebop-era (1940s)
development per the same source — using it as the *default* tonic here would be anachronistic.

**Extensions and passing chords — `cited`.** Dominant 9ths/13ths were standard dance-band
vocabulary by the 1930s (Fletcher Henderson-style charts); diminished passing chords
(I–♯I°7–ii7, and secondary dominants like V7/V) are well-documented connective devices in
the era's standards (general jazz-theory pedagogy; cited example is 1937's "Have You Met
Miss Jones," just past this brief's window but the same vocabulary).

**Form — `cited`.** AABA 32-bar form was "the principal form" of American popular song
from ≈1925–26, dominant "into the 1950s" (Wikipedia "Thirty-two-bar form," itself flagged
there as short on hard statistics). The bridge's circle-of-fifths dominant chain,
III7–VI7–II7–V7 (two bars each), is nicknamed the "Sears-Roebuck bridge," canonically
exemplified by "I Got Rhythm" (1930); the nickname reads as later shorthand, not 1930s
usage (Wikipedia "Rhythm changes").

**Romantic/light-classical borrowings — `cited`, gap flagged.** Hotel/theater dance
orchestras inherited string-forward writing from salon and operetta tradition — the
German Tanzorchester "foregrounded lush string melodies" over reed/brass riffs, and
"light music" broadly kept "diatonic [harmony] with tasteful color." Minor-iv "backdoor"
plagal motion and chromatic mediants did **not** turn up as documented period-typical
devices — treat both as occasional `designed-space` color, not core vocabulary.

**Ellington, briefly — `cited`.** "Sophisticated Lady" (1932) and "Solitude" (1934) both
build A sections from descending chromatic dominant/diminished chains — advanced relative
to standard stock-chart harmony of the same years. Useful as an upper bound on how far the
era's harmony *could* reach, not as the ballroom-card default.

## 7. Foxtrot/ballroom performance practice, 1930–1936

**Tempo — `cited`.** Modern ISTD-descended Slow Foxtrot (International Style) runs
112–120 bpm (28–30 bars/min); American-style foxtrot 120–136 bpm; social-dance sources
put ~130 bpm as a comfortable floor-filler with 116 bpm a practical lower bound. 1920s
foxtrot reportedly ran faster still (~32–34 bars/min ≈ 128–136 bpm), tempos "quietly
adjusted downward over decades." Victor Silvester formed his Ballroom Orchestra in 1935
specifically because existing dance records weren't tempo-consistent enough, conforming
recordings to ISTD-recommended bpm ("slow, slow, quick-quick-slow"). **`inferred`:** this
pass did not independently confirm a numeric bpm from Silvester's own recordings — treat
112–120 bpm as its traceable modern descendant, not a period-primary number.

**Instrumentation — `cited`.** Whiteman's orchestra grew from ~16 musicians (early '20s)
to ~26 (mid-'20s, adding a full string section), reportedly as many as 35 at points later
in the decade, settling at 27 + vocalist by 1938 — pioneering the saxophone section as
brass's structural equal and favoring notated arrangement over improvisation (3,000+
charts written). Whiteman's own writing splits concert jazz orchestra from dance
orchestra: "For a dance orchestra, eight violins are an unnecessary number of strings...
one of the pianos may be omitted and an extra banjo added" for its "clear, snappy"
rhythmic drive (Whiteman & McBride, *Saturday Evening Post*, 1926) — strings were already
trimmed for *dance* function versus concert showcase. British hotel bands (Savoy Hotel
Orpheans under Carroll Gibbons from 1931; Ambrose at the May Fair 1927–39) ran genteel,
string-inclusive 12–16-piece lineups distinct from the harder Kansas City/Harlem lineage.
Broadway pit orchestras grew larger across the era too; no reliable counts surfaced — flagged thin.

**Strings' role — `cited` + `inferred`.** Standard section functions — sustained pads
holding harmony underneath melody and rhythm, countermelody, unison doubling of the lead
— read directly onto the era's dance-orchestra string writing given the salon/operetta lineage above.

## 8. Mix aesthetics of the era

**`cited`.** Electrical recording (from 1925, Western Electric/Bell Labs) widened
frequency response from a ≈250–2500 Hz acoustic-horn ceiling to ≈50–6000 Hz. Sessions ran
on very few microphones — large ensemble/choral dates "rarely more than six mics," often
effectively one — balance achieved by physically positioning players, not a console
fader; direct-to-disc, no multitrack, through the mid-1930s. The RCA Type 44 ribbon mic
(1931, widely adopted ~1933) gave a focused figure-8 pattern that tamed room reverberation
and became the decade's dominant studio/broadcast mic. Crooning (Bing Crosby et al.)
exploited the same mic advances for close, warm, intimate vocal proximity in place of the
belted projection acoustic-era singers needed.

**`designed-space`.** Extend that forward-intimate treatment to piano and lead voices —
dry-ish, upfront — while string pads and horn textures sit back with more room tone,
matching a single-perspective era mix rather than a modern multitrack spread.
